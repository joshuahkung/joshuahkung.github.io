This week was mostly TA responsibilities and scoping out what the next coordination domain should look like, now that the basketball run sim has a decent set of policies behind it.

Domain-wise, everything I've tested so far has been fairly "flat" — a group of peers with no clear hierarchy. I wanted to find a scenario with a real manager/organizer role: someone making calls for people who have different incentives and personas, closer to project management than casual group planning. I looked at a few directions: project management tools like Trello, Jira, or Asana coordinating across teams toward a shared timeline (Jira in particular models by team, but real coordination is messier than that clean structure suggests); negotiation scenarios built around BATNA and payoff matrices, where people wrongly assume every outcome is zero-sum when mutual gains are often possible — games like Werewolf, Secret Hitler, Avalon, or Survivor could work well here; classic game-theory setups like the ultimatum game or prisoner's dilemma; party planning; and TA staffing, which I ended up fleshing out in more detail.

The TA staffing example got concrete fast: students want office hours the night before Friday's homework deadline, but TAs — who are also students — don't want to work Thursdays, even though that's exactly when demand is highest. Late sign-ups get stuck with leftover slots they don't want and have to negotiate swaps among themselves. Ideally this creates a genuine conflict that can't resolve itself without the coordinator stepping in — which is exactly the kind of situation I want the system to handle well. A related, lower-priority problem: people flake right before an assignment is due, so grading coverage needs a fallback plan too.

Also went back to Tao's list of tasks the lab has considered in the past — HCI seminar slot allocation, seminar hosting, workshop hosting, CPT/OPT applications, defense timeline tracking, department retreat planning — good reference material for what makes a task "slow-burning and people-dependent" enough to be worth simulating, versus tasks that are cleaner but less socially embedded (like the Jumpstarter paper's more individual-goal-oriented task list).

**Next steps:** pick one of these new domains and actually build it out, rather than just scoping options.
