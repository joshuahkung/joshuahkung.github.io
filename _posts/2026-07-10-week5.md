This week I went back to fixing the dashboard itself: the status pills weren't reliably reflecting the current state of a run on every update, and flagging on the response view needed to be clearer.

The bigger issue I ran into was realism. The simulation wasn't behaving the way real coordination actually works, because in practice people don't respond promptly — a real coordinator only acts because of a hard deadline or because people have gone silent long enough that someone has to make a call. Right now the sim doesn't create that same pressure, so the coordinator has less reason to actually intervene.

I looked at two ways to fix this. One option is to just speed up simulated time — compress six real hours into one simulated minute — so that silence accumulates fast enough to force a decision. The other is to make simulated participants check their messages at staggered, realistic intervals instead of responding instantly, so gaps in response times emerge naturally rather than needing to be sped through.

On the dashboard side, I want better visibility into how far off-track a given run is and who the actual bottleneck is — if there's a real problem, I should be able to see who has slack and could be reassigned or reached out to. I also want to handle a few messier real-world cases better: people who list multiple available days, questions that are more open-ended than a strict yes/no/maybe, and clearer per-person notes (e.g. "can't do Friday," "has a flight that day"). I'd also like a table tracking the full lifecycle of a message — sent, confirmed, date given — and to distinguish between junior and senior participants, since they probably shouldn't be treated identically by the coordinator's policies.

**Next steps:** pick one of the two realism fixes (accelerated time vs. staggered checking) and actually implement it, since right now the sim is too forgiving to really test whether the coordinator handles pressure well.
